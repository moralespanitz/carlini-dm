# Carlini Decision Framework

A structured decision framework for evaluating ideas, projects, and priorities — adapted from Nicholas Carlini's methodology for high-impact work.

> "One excellent paper is worth a thousand mediocre ones, and takes less time to write." — Carlini

## How to Use This Skill

When invoked, ask the user to describe what they're deciding — an idea to pursue, a project to continue or kill, or a set of options to prioritize. Then walk through the seven dimensions below, asking one targeted question per dimension. Keep it conversational, not bureaucratic.

For detailed criteria and scoring rubrics, see [references/framework.md](references/framework.md).

---

## The Seven Dimensions

### 1. Impact
**Question to ask:** "If this succeeds, what specifically changes? Who cares, and why now?"

Carlini's test: write your best-case conclusion before doing any work. If the only thing you can say is "the number went up by 2%", it probably doesn't matter. The answer should be articulable in one sentence that would make a peer say "I wish I'd done that."

Red flags: "it would be useful", "fills a gap in the literature", no concrete beneficiary.

---

### 2. Uniqueness
**Question to ask:** "What makes this something only you — or only your team — could do right now?"

Carlini's test: ideas are cheap; execution is hard. How many months ahead are you of the next person who would do this? If the answer is "anyone could do this next week", the contribution is thin. Aim for problems where your head start is meaningful.

Red flags: "it's not done yet", "I have time", no articulated edge.

---

### 3. Comparative Advantage
**Question to ask:** "Does this play to your specific strengths — the corner of the skill space where you might be world-class?"

Carlini's framework: find the high-dimensional corner where your particular mix of skills is unusually suited. Theory-builder or problem-solver? Brings fields together or drives rigorous execution in one? The best work often comes from this self-knowledge.

Red flags: "I could learn it", "it's a hot area so I should do it", doing it because someone else is.

---

### 4. De-risking
**Question to ask:** "What's the riskiest assumption? Have you tested it yet — or are you working on the easy parts first?"

Carlini's principle: start with the sub-problem most likely to fail. Don't build the polished version when a prototype will tell you whether the core idea works. Fail fast applies to research and engineering alike.

Red flags: months of work with no test of the core bet, "I'll get to the hard part later."

---

### 5. Kill / Pivot Signals
**Question to ask:** "If you're honest — is this working? Or are you in a sunk-cost spiral?"

Carlini's heuristic: kill papers (and projects) that aren't working. Kill them not because they're bad ideas, but because opportunity cost is real. The exponential distribution of impact means even a 90%-done mediocre project should sometimes be abandoned for a 10%-started great one.

Red flags: "I've put too much in to stop", "it's almost there", rationalizing diminishing returns.

---

### 6. Timing
**Question to ask:** "Is the world ready for this? Could you be too early — or is the window closing?"

Carlini's observation: ideas that are too early get rejected not because they're wrong, but because reviewers/stakeholders don't yet believe the premise. Being six months earlier than the field on an important problem can have outsized impact. Being six months too late means you're following.

Red flags: "everyone is doing this now" (late), "no one gets why this matters yet" (possibly too early — or possibly wrong).

---

### 7. Effort-to-Impact Ratio
**Question to ask:** "Is this the maximal version of this project? Or are you under/over-investing relative to what the idea deserves?"

Carlini's principle: great papers don't have poorly performed experiments. Put in an unreasonable amount of effort on the things that matter. But also: don't leave obvious improvements undone that a reader will notice. The paper/project should be at a local optimum — satisfying, not exhausting.

Red flags: "good enough to ship", important questions left unanswered, OR scope creep that dilutes the core idea.

---

## Delivering the Recommendation

After walking through the dimensions, synthesize:

1. **Top 2-3 strongest signals** (positive or negative)
2. **One-sentence verdict**: Continue / Kill / Pivot / De-risk first
3. **One concrete next action** — the single most important thing to do or test

Don't hedge. Carlini's tone: direct, honest, focused on what actually matters. If the signals are mixed, say so — but still give a recommendation.

---

## What This Framework Is Not

- Not a checklist to justify work you've already decided to do
- Not a way to make every idea sound good
- Not a substitute for taste — it helps articulate taste, not replace it

> "Focus on the distribution. The award, if it comes, is just someone noticing where your distribution ended up." — Carlini
